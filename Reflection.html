Community-Engaged Learning Reflections

Initial thoughts and final reflections on the Hope Haven project.

Initial Reflection Web Page

What do you think community-engaged learning is?

We understand community-engaged learning as a type of education where students work directly with real people, organizations, or community needs while learning course content. Instead of only studying ideas in a classroom, we apply our skills to real problems that matter to others. In this project, we are using technology and web design to support people who may be facing homelessness, food insecurity, or other hardships.

Through online research, we learned that community-engaged learning is meant to be mutually beneficial. The community receives useful support, and students gain practical experience, empathy, and a deeper understanding of social issues.

Have you ever participated in any type of community service?

We have participated in community service in a few small ways, such as helping with local donation drives and school-based volunteer events. These activities showed us that even simple actions, like organizing supplies or sharing information, can make life easier for someone else. They also helped us understand how much planning and teamwork are needed behind the scenes.

How do you feel about working on a community-engagement project?

We feel both excited and responsible about working on a community-engagement project. It is exciting because our work has the potential to reach real people who might use this website during a difficult time. At the same time, we feel a sense of responsibility to present information clearly, respectfully, and accurately so that users are not misled or confused.

Designing and developing a website for the community helps us see our engineering and computing skills as tools for service, not just as technical tasks or assignments.

In which ways can people use the engineering design process as a tool to solve problems and improve life?

The engineering design process gives us a structured way to understand a problem, explore possible solutions, build prototypes, and improve them based on feedback. When this process is used with real community needs, it can produce tools that make everyday life safer, easier, or more dignified. For example, our project uses the design process to plan features that help people search for food and shelter resources.

By talking with partners, testing our ideas, and revising our designs, we can make sure our solutions are realistic, respectful, and accessible to the people who will use them.

What information do I need?

To complete this project well, we need information about how local organizations serve people who are homeless or food insecure, and what types of resources people search for the most. We also need to understand what kind of language and design is most helpful and respectful when presenting sensitive topics.

We will look for this information on nonprofit and government websites, through any materials shared by our community partners, and by reviewing examples of other successful community-focused websites.

Final Reflection Web Page

Strengths discovered during the project

During this community-engagement project, we discovered that we are good at organizing information and breaking complex ideas into simple, clear steps for users. We also realized that we can adapt quickly when technical or design challenges appear, and we are comfortable learning new tools when they are needed to support the project.

Challenges in dealing with other people

One challenge was coordinating schedules and communication styles with team members and partners. Sometimes we had different ideas about priorities or design choices. Learning to listen carefully, explain our own ideas clearly, and compromise when needed was an important part of the experience. It taught us that working on community projects requires patience and respect for many different perspectives.

Qualities to work on in the future

We learned that we would like to keep improving our time management and our ability to ask questions early when we are unsure about requirements. We also want to become more confident in speaking with community partners so that we can better understand their expectations and share progress in a professional way.

Memorable things learned about partner organizations

Through this project, we learned that organizations serving people experiencing homelessness or food insecurity do much more than provide basic services. Many of them offer counseling, job assistance, education programs, and long-term support to help people rebuild stability. Some partners run both food pantries and programs that teach budgeting or job skills so that families can move toward a more secure future.

We were impressed by how much planning, coordination, and passion is required to keep these programs running every day.

What we learned about the people served

One important lesson was that people who use these services come from many different backgrounds. Some are working but still cannot afford housing or food, and others are dealing with health issues, family changes, or unexpected crises. This reminded us that anyone can need help at some point, and that it is important not to make assumptions or judgments about people based on their situation.

Something we wish more people knew

We wish more people knew how powerful small acts of support can be, such as sharing accurate information, volunteering a few hours a month, or donating basic items. We also wish more people understood that clear, accessible technology like the Hope Haven website can make a real difference when someone is searching for help in a stressful moment.

This project showed us that combining technical skills with empathy can create tools that truly support the community.
